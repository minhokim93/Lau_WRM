Dynamic Evolution of Pyrogeography

Fire regimes are changing but our understanding of pyrogeography tends to be static. We study the dynamic evolution of pyromes over time to see how they cluster and transition — revealing intensifying trends across 17.2% of global grid cells.

Background What is a Pyrome?

A pyrome is a fire regime — a cluster of landscape cells that share similar wildfire characteristics. Unlike static maps, pyromes should be understood as dynamic: they shift as climate, land use, and fuel loads change.

This project uses unsupervised machine learning on the Global Fire Atlas (2002–2025) to cluster five distinct pyrome classes at 1-degree spatial resolution for every four-year interval. The five key variables driving clustering are: fire frequency, size, expansion rate, duration, and perimeter.

Data is sourced from the Global Fire Atlas at 0.25-degree resolution derived from satellite imagery (Andela et al., 2019).

5 Pyrome Classes
P0 — Non-Active
P1 — Low Activity
P2 — Moderate / Transition
P3 — Moderate to Severe
P4 — High Activity
Method Clustering Pyromes via ML

Five regimes are clustered to 1-degree spatial resolution using unsupervised learning (k-means) for every four-year interval from 2002 to 2025. Each pyrome possesses distinct characteristics related to wildfire activity across the five key dimensions.

There are six sets of pyromes (one per interval). When compared over time, we observe how pyromes evolve in space and time — then study how they transition from one class to another between intervals.

Finding Pyrome Stability & Transitions

Most pyromes tend to remain stable. Transitions occur most frequently between Non-Active, Low, and Moderate (P0, P1, P2). Some transitions occur in Moderate/Severe (P3), while fewer occur in High Activity (P4).

Pyrome stability is measured by counting all transitions occurring between 2002 and 2025. More transitions correspond to low stability; fewer transitions correspond to high stability.

Finding Hotspots & Coldspots of Change

Pyrome classes are weighted numerically (P0=0 to P4=4). Transitions compute the difference in assigned values (e.g., P0→P4 yields +4). Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) identify clusters of intensifying and recovering regions.

Hotspots indicate regions where pyromes are consistently transitioning to higher activity classes over time. Coldspots represent regions recovering toward lower activity classes.

More regions (17.2% of grid cells) are transitioning into more intensifying pyromes in recent years — a near 10% increase in the past two decades. Hotspots exist globally, mainly in South Africa, Australia, North America, South America, and Eastern Europe-Asia.
